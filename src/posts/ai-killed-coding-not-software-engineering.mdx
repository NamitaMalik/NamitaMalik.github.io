# AI Killed Coding, Not Software Engineering

The panic around AI replacing developers comes from a misunderstanding that existed long before AI arrived:

Too many people confused coding with software engineering.

Coding was always just one part of the job. Important, yes. But only one part.

Software engineering was never simply about turning requirements into lines of code.

It was about understanding problems, designing solutions, navigating constraints, managing risk, and delivering outcomes that actually work.

AI is exposing that difference.

## Coding Was Never the Whole Job

A feature does not begin when someone opens an editor.

It begins with questions:

* What problem are we solving?
* What are the business goals?
* What are the edge cases?
* What systems will this affect?
* What are the security, performance, and operational risks?
* Is this even the right feature to build?

Then comes planning:

* How should it be designed?
* What trade-offs are acceptable?
* What dependencies exist?
* How will it scale?
* How will it be tested and observed?

Only then comes implementation.

Whether that implementation is typed by human hands or generated with AI is increasingly secondary.

## Output vs Outcome

AI can help produce output.

* code
* tests
* documentation
* refactors
* scaffolding

But outcomes are still owned by humans.

* solving the real problem
* shipping safely
* meeting customer needs
* maintaining quality
* protecting reliability
* avoiding long-term messes
* making sound trade-offs

That responsibility does not disappear because a model wrote the function.

When code generation becomes easier, poor thinking becomes more expensive.

Bad requirements become faster bad products.
Weak architecture becomes faster technical debt.
Shallow understanding becomes faster chaos.

Domain knowledge, judgement, communication, and systems thinking become the real differentiators.

## Architecture Matters More Than Ever

As code becomes cheaper to produce, architecture becomes more expensive to get wrong.

AI can generate code quickly. But it still relies on what you ask it to build — and how clearly you define it.

Without a clear architectural direction, AI doesn’t reduce complexity. It amplifies it.

Prompt-driven development often hides architectural decisions:

* data models are guessed
* boundaries are blurred
* scalability is an afterthought
* non-functional requirements are ignored

The result is accidental architecture.

And accidental architecture does not scale.

This is where clarity in your head — and your ability to translate that clarity into specs — becomes a core engineering skill.

In many ways, architecture is just spec-driven thinking at system level.

## From Specs to Behavior: SDD + TDD

Even traditional practices like Test-Driven Development (TDD) gain new relevance.

TDD forces you to define behavior before implementation.
It creates guardrails for AI-generated code.
It keeps the system honest.

But in reality, most organisations are already practicing some form of Spec-Driven Development (SDD) — through requirements, tickets, acceptance criteria, and design docs.

The difference now is not adoption. It’s quality.

When AI starts generating the implementation, weak specs don’t get exposed slowly.
They get executed immediately.

Vague requirements → confidently wrong systems.
Incomplete specs → inconsistent architectures.

This makes the relationship between the two explicit:

* SDD defines **intent (what and why)**
* TDD validates **behavior (how it works)**

AI amplifies both — or breaks both.

In a world where code is abundant, clarity of intent and correctness of behavior become the real constraints.

We were always writing specs.
Now we actually have to mean them.

## I’ve Seen This Before

I’ve personally seen multiple failures long before AI, where teams equated coding with software engineering.

Projects shipped code but lacked clarity.
Features were built without fully understanding the problem.
Teams optimised for implementation speed while ignoring architecture, ownership, and long-term impact.

The result was predictable: fragile systems, rework, and missed expectations.

AI did not create this problem. It just makes it easier to scale it.

## The Bigger Risk: Bad AI Adoption

My real concern is not AI replacing engineers.

It is organizations rushing AI adoption without engineering maturity.

Pushing AI left, right, and centre just to appear innovative can create:

* broken delivery processes
* lower quality standards
* security gaps
* duplicated code at scale
* hidden technical debt
* confused ownership
* junior teams with less learning depth
* more output, less value

If companies kill healthy engineering processes in the name of speed, they may create bigger problems than the ones AI promised to solve.

## AI as a Thought Partner, Not a Replacement

One of the best uses of AI today is in thinking, not just coding.

You can:

* challenge your architecture
* explore trade-offs
* simulate failure scenarios
* get multiple perspectives quickly

In many ways, this is far better than rubber ducking.

But it still does not replace peer review.

AI can broaden your thinking.
Peers ground it in reality.

That balance still matters.

## A Note on AI in Practice

AI is already helping in places like code reviews.

But it still cannot compete with the context in an engineer’s head — why something exists, what constraints shaped it, what failed before, and what must not fail again.

AI can review code.

Engineers review intent.

## Tools Are Changing, Engineering Is Not

Tools have always evolved.

From manual deployments to CI/CD.
From monoliths to distributed systems.
From autocomplete to generative AI.
From copilots to agents.

The pace is faster now, but the pattern is not new.

Software engineering still remains:

* solving problems
* making sound decisions
* managing complexity
* balancing trade-offs
* delivering outcomes responsibly

## The Future Belongs to Engineers

The winners will not be people who equated coding with engineering.

They will be people who understood that coding was only ever one tool.

AI may generate the output.

But engineers are still responsible for the outcome.
